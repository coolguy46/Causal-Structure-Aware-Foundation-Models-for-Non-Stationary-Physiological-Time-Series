Writing Stack
For the paper itself use Overleaf with the official NeurIPS 2026 LaTeX template — download it from the NeurIPS website the moment it drops, usually February or March. Overleaf's free tier is enough for one paper. Use Git integration in Overleaf so you have version history and can roll back if you break something.
For reference management use Zotero, which is free. Install the browser extension so you can one-click import papers from Google Scholar, Semantic Scholar, and arXiv. Export your library as a .bib file and sync it with your Overleaf project. Never manually type a citation — every single one should come from Zotero to avoid formatting errors.
For figure creation use matplotlib for all data figures — loss curves, ablation bar charts, benchmark comparison tables rendered as heatmaps. Use the neurips_style matplotlib rc params so your figures match the paper font. For architecture diagrams use draw.io or Inkscape to produce clean SVGs, then export to PDF for LaTeX inclusion. Never include raster PNG figures in a NeurIPS paper — always PDF or EPS for vector crispness.
For AI writing assistance Claude or GPT-4 are both fine for prose cleanup, rephrasing, and transitions. Use them on individual paragraphs, not full sections. Always rewrite AI output in your own words before it goes into the paper, and verify every factual claim manually. The related work section especially — never let AI write citations you haven't personally read.
For grammar and clarity use Grammarly on top of AI assistance. For academic register specifically, the Hemingway Editor is useful for flagging sentences that are too long or convoluted — NeurIPS reviewers appreciate tight, direct prose.

Title — be specific and claim-forward. Something like "Causal Structure-Aware Foundation Models for Non-Stationary Physiological Time Series" works. Avoid vague words like "novel" or "towards" in your title — reviewers dislike them.
Abstract — half a page, strictly. Four things only: the problem and why it matters, the gap in existing work, your specific contribution, and your strongest result. Write this last after everything else is done.
1. Introduction — 1.5 pages
Open with the concrete clinical motivation — biosignal models fail to generalize across subjects and devices, and this is a safety-critical problem. Immediately follow with why this happens mechanistically — existing models learn spurious correlations rather than causal structure. Then state your contributions as a numbered list, explicitly. NeurIPS reviewers scan the intro for this list. Your list should have exactly three items: the architecture contribution (graph-conditioned transformer), the theoretical contribution (generalization bound), and the empirical contribution (zero-shot transfer results). Close with a one-paragraph overview of the paper structure.
2. Related Work — 1 page
Cover exactly four clusters of prior work: biosignal foundation models (BrainOmni, THD-BAR, SPICED from NeurIPS 2025 — explain why each is insufficient), causal representation learning (IRM, causally-regularized models — explain what they lack for time-varying graphs), time series transformers (PatchTST, TimesNet — explain why flat attention is wrong for structured signals), and graph neural networks for biosignals (existing work using static graphs — explain why static graphs fail for non-stationary physiology). Be crisp — one paragraph per cluster, no more.
3. Problem Formulation — 0.5 pages
Define your data formally. A multi-channel biosignal is a matrix X ∈ R^(C×T) where C is channels and T is time steps. Define the time-varying structural causal model (SCM) assumption — the underlying data-generating process is a DAG G_t that changes with physiological state. Define what zero-shot transfer means formally in your setting. This section is short but critical — reviewers will refer back to your notation throughout.
4. Method — 2.5 pages
This is the core of the paper and gets the most space. Structure it as four subsections. First the spectral tokenizer — describe the per-band STFT, the projection to d_token dimensions, the positional encoding scheme with channel ID and band ID embeddings. Include the key equation for token construction. Second the causal graph inference module — describe the pairwise edge scoring, the straight-through sparsification, and the DAG constraint. Include the adjacency matrix equation. Third the graph-conditioned transformer — describe exactly how the adjacency matrix becomes the attention mask, the additive masking scheme, and the non-stationarity adapter design. Include the masked attention equation. Fourth the joint training objective — write out all three loss terms formally, define all lambda hyperparameters, and give the intuition for why the causal consistency term enforces generalization.
5. Theoretical Analysis — 0.5 pages
State Theorem 1 formally: under assumptions (A1)-(A5), the Rademacher complexity of graph-conditioned attention satisfies R_n(F_G^L) = O(rho * B_x * alpha^L * L * sqrt(k * d_h) / sqrt(n)), versus R_n(F_full^L) = O(... sqrt(N * d_h) / sqrt(n)) for full attention, where k is max in-degree and N is total tokens. The improvement ratio is sqrt(k/N). State all five assumptions explicitly with justification. Give: Lemma 1 (single-head Lipschitz constant under graph mask — attention output depends on at most k parents), Lemma 2 (per-layer covering number reduction from N^2*d_h to N*k*d_h), then the main proof via Dudley's entropy integral and the deep network peeling technique of Golowich et al. (2018). State the graph estimation extension: if learned graph has Hamming error delta_H, effective degree becomes k + delta_H*N, and bound degrades gracefully. Conclude with the connection to IRM — the causal consistency loss enforces a strictly stronger condition (Pearl's truncated factorization under intervention) than environment-level invariance. Be honest: the bound scales as alpha^L (exponential in depth, standard for spectral-norm bounds), and causal sufficiency is assumed. Point to appendix for full proof.
6. Experiments — 2 pages
Structure this as five subsections. First datasets and setup — describe Sleep-EDF (2ch EEG, 5-class sleep staging), CHB-MIT (23ch EEG, seizure detection), and PTB-XL (12-lead ECG, 5-class arrhythmia), subject counts, train/val/test split strategy (subject-stratified, never random), evaluation metrics (F1, AUROC, ECE). Second main results (Table 1 + Fig 1) — 6 models on all 3 datasets across all metrics, 3 random seeds with mean±std. Your 5 baselines: PatchTST, Vanilla Transformer, Static GNN, Correlation Graph, Raw Waveform. Third within-modality transfer (Table 3 + Fig 5) — zero-shot cross-dataset transfer heatmap showing EEG→EEG generalization. Note: cross-modality transfer (EEG→ECG) is not claimed since causal graphs differ across organs. Fourth ablation study (Table 2 + Fig 4) — 7 loss configurations (all, no_causal, no_spectral, no_recon, no_dag, no_sparse, cls_only) + λ_causal sensitivity curve. Fifth causal validation — three sub-figures: (a) synthetic graph recovery on linear + nonlinear VAR processes (Table 4 + Fig 6a), (b) learned causal graphs on real data (Fig 2) with comparison to known neurophysiology, (c) interventional validation showing do(X_i:=0) only changes descendants (Fig 3). Sixth theoretical verification (Fig 6a+6b + Fig 7) — empirical generalization gap vs Theorem 1 bound, bound scaling with n, and sparsity vs generalization scatter. Seventh calibration (Fig 8) — reliability diagrams for all 3 datasets. Statistical significance (paired t-tests, Cohen's d) reported for all main comparisons.
7. Conclusion — 0.25 pages
Three paragraphs. What you did, what it shows, and what the honest limitations are. Always include limitations — NeurIPS reviewers specifically look for this and absence of a limitations discussion is a red flag. Your main limitation is that the SCM assumption may not hold perfectly for all physiological signals, and that your adapter requires a small amount of target-domain data.
References — unlimited pages, not counted toward the 9-page limit. Every citation must be in consistent NeurIPS bib format. Use the official bib entries from Semantic Scholar rather than auto-generated ones from Google Scholar, which often have formatting errors.
Appendix — unlimited pages, not counted. Put your full theorem proof here, extended ablation tables, dataset preprocessing details, hyperparameter tables, and your reproducibility checklist. The NeurIPS reproducibility checklist is mandatory and must be completed honestly — it asks about code release, dataset licenses, compute budget, and random seeds.

One final note on process
Write section 4 (Method) first, not the introduction. You understand your method best right now — get it on paper while it's fresh. Write the introduction and abstract last, after you have your experimental results, because you cannot honestly write "our model achieves X" until X exists.
